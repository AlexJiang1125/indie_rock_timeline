The Birth of Indie: How Hardcore and Jangle Pop Converged into C86
1982 — 1986
This is the first era where I feel like it's impossible to not separate things by their region. Basically, things are moving so differently across both sides of the pond. On the US side, well...it's giving whole house mad. Hardcore rose above (or...under?) the ashes of punk as the true dominating genre for the first few years of 80s. They are usually fast, aggressive and unpolished with deep roots in their local scenes, with their manifestos being anti-commercial, anti-establishment and anti-hipsters. As for the hipsters in New York, the no wave movement formed against the polished music of New wave, using abstract ideas to revolt any intentions of writing a good melody line. For a few years, "making good music" seemed to not be the focus of the US indie. Eventually, people began to learn how to blend melodies into the chaos and crazy rhythms of hardcore punk, with the Replacements and Husker Du becoming the leading sounds of this era; On the other side, bands like the Descendants and Violent Femmes sang so freely about the awkwardness and angst of being an adolescent, that it became blueprints for later genres like emocore. Things are far more accessible in the UK, the pop sensibility is still there, mostly because their power-pop influenced, guitar based "indie" sound is developed as a response to the highly produced, synth-heavy New-wave and later New Romantics movements.

Alt-Country (Part II: 2000s)
Aesthetic-wise, alt-country maintained its style in this decade, blending folk, rock and country elements and pretty much carried on the greatness in the 1990s . Artists like Gillian Welch, Bill Callahan, Wilco and Silver Jews built upon their styles and went on to release great records. Other artists like Jason Molina and Drive-by-truckers emerged out of the scene and made some of the defining records of this decade. Some artists would become a mainstay in this genre, but the craze gradually died down during the latter part of the decade due to more electronic and pop-focused indie rock acts taking over the spotlight and the derogative term "dad-rock" being coined to describe this genre. Moreover, quite a few key artists slowed down with the outputs or unfortunately passed away. Nevertheless, many records in this decade would proven to be influential in the alt-country revival two decades later.

Shoegaze (Part II: 2000s)
Shoegaze entered a fallow period for the better part of the 2000s. Just like Britpop completely dominated Shoegaze in the 90s UK, the revival of garage- and punk rock, and later indie rock took up the whole space. The big Shoegaze acts in the 1990s went into hiatus, while the existing new ones remained on the fringes. Nevertheless, this decade saw Shoegaze blending into other genres like post-punk (Autolux, Blonde Redhead), indie pop (The Radio Dept.), electronica (Sweet Trip) and ambient (M83). Things picked up again at the end of the decade, with a new generation of acts (Deerhunter, A Sunny Day in Glasgow) bringing in a new sound, exploring the elements of Shoegaze to evoke nostalgia feelings.
